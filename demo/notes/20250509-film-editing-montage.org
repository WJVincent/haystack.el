* Eisenstein's montage theory

Sergei Eisenstein argued that the essence of cinema is not the
individual shot but the collision between shots.  Meaning is created
by juxtaposition, not by content.

Five types of montage:

1. *Metric* — cuts based on absolute length (every N frames).
   Creates rhythm through regularity.
2. *Rhythmic* — cuts based on content rhythm, not frame count.
   The movement within the shot determines the cut point.
3. *Tonal* — cuts based on the emotional tone of each shot.
   Light vs. dark, sharp vs. soft.
4. *Overtonal* — combination of metric, rhythmic, and tonal
   simultaneously.  The most complex form.
5. *Intellectual* — juxtaposition of images to create an abstract
   idea.  The Odessa Steps sequence in /Battleship Potemkin/ is the
   canonical example.

* The Kuleshov effect

Lev Kuleshov demonstrated that the same shot of an actor's face,
intercut with different images (a bowl of soup, a child's coffin, a
woman), produces different emotional responses in the viewer.  The
composition of meaning happens in the edit, not in the performance.

This is relevant to any system that presents information through
juxtaposition.  A search results buffer is a montage: each result
line is a "shot," and the meaning of the whole buffer emerges from
their sequence and proximity.  Filtering is re-editing — you are
cutting the montage down to the essential shots.

* Walter Murch — "In the Blink of an Eye"

Murch's rule of six for a good cut (in order of priority):

1. Emotion — does the cut serve the emotion of the scene?
2. Story — does it advance the story?
3. Rhythm — does it feel right in terms of pacing?
4. Eye-trace — does the viewer's eye land in the right place?
5. 2D plane — does the composition work on screen?
6. 3D space — is the spatial continuity maintained?

He is willing to sacrifice any lower priority for a higher one.
Spatial continuity can be broken if it serves the emotion.

* Synthesis

Montage and progressive filtering share a structure: you start with
raw material (footage / search results), and through a series of
cuts (edits / filters), you arrive at a composition that means more
than the sum of its parts.  The composition is the insight.

The difference: film editing is authorial (the editor decides the
sequence), while search filtering is exploratory (the user discovers
the sequence).  But both are acts of synthesis through elimination.
