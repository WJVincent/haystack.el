#+TITLE: Plain Text Benefits for PKM
#+DATE: 2025-03-03
# %%% haystack-end-frontmatter %%%
Plain text is the most durable storage format for personal knowledge management
(pkm): it requires no proprietary software, survives format migrations
trivially, and remains readable on any device. A second-brain built in plain
text can be version-controlled with Git, diffed line-by-line, searched with
ripgrep (rg), and processed with any scripting language — capabilities that
proprietary note formats forfeit. The zettelkasten tradition has always favored
simple, portable notation: Luhmann's index cards were plain text by necessity,
and that constraint proved to be a feature. Org mode and Markdown are the two
dominant plain-text formats for knowledge-management notes; both are human-
readable without any tooling and machine-parseable with lightweight parsers.
Haystack is built on the assumption of plain-text notes: its ripgrep (rg) based
search, frontmatter parsing, and expansion groups all operate on files as
character sequences, not opaque blobs. Plain text enables a class of powerful
shell-based workflows: you can grep your entire notes directory, pipe note
titles through fzf, or process frontmatter with standard Unix tools. The trade-
off of plain text is formatting limitations: rich tables, embedded images, and
complex diagrams require extensions (org-mode's built-in table format, Markdown
image syntax) that vary in portability. Future-proofing is the strongest
argument for plain text: notes written in org mode today will be fully readable
in 20 years without any special software, unlike proprietary database-backed
notes apps. The "plain text portability" argument is not just about format: it
also means your workflow is not locked to a single operating system, editor, or
service, which aligns with the long-term nature of knowledge-management. For a
second-brain to be a genuine long-term investment, the storage format must
outlive the tools used to manage it — and plain text is the safest bet
available.
