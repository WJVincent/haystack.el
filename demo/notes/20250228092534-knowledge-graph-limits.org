#+TITLE: Knowledge Graph Limits
#+DATE: 2025-02-28
# %%% haystack-end-frontmatter %%%
Knowledge graphs — networks where notes (zettel) are nodes and explicit links
are edges — are a compelling model for personal knowledge management (pkm), but
they have practical limitations that are often underappreciated. The maintenance
burden is the first limitation: every link must be created and kept up to date
manually; a large corpus with thousands of nodes and edges quickly becomes
expensive to curate accurately. Graph visualization, while visually impressive,
rarely provides actionable insight: a dense hairball of 500 interconnected nodes
tells you less about your knowledge than a well-maintained set of evergreen
notes. Link rot is a significant problem — as notes are renamed, split, or
merged, hard-coded links break silently and must be repaired through either
tooling (org-roam handles this with its ID-based system) or discipline. The
vocabulary-gap problem is particularly acute for graphs: if you linked a note
about "retrieval" but later search for "search," the link is invisible unless
you also indexed the synonym. Haystack's design explicitly sidesteps graph
maintenance by relying on full-text search (rg/ripgrep) and expansion groups for
synonym handling; the system finds connections dynamically rather than requiring
you to declare them in advance. Emergent connections — ideas that only become
related after both notes exist — are systematically harder to capture in a link-
based graph than in a search-based system, because they require retroactive link
creation. The second-brain literature sometimes overstates graph density as a
quality signal; a knowledge-management system's value comes from retrieval
quality and insight generation, not from having many links. For small corpora
(under a few hundred notes), graph-based systems like org-roam work well; for
larger corpora, the maintenance overhead often degrades into a partially stale
graph that is less reliable than fresh search. The most robust pkm systems
combine both approaches: explicit links for the most important relationships,
and full-text search for everything else, using each where it has a comparative
advantage.
