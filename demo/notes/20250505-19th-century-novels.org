* Reading notes

** Dostoevsky — /Crime and Punishment/

Raskolnikov's theory of the "extraordinary man" is a theory about
categorization.  He divides humanity into ordinary and extraordinary,
then tries to act on the classification.  The novel is about what
happens when your categories are wrong.

The structure mirrors the argument: Part I is the crime (acting on
theory), Parts II–VI are the punishment (reality refusing to fit the
theory).  The synthesis comes only at the very end, in the epilogue,
and it is ambiguous.

** Dostoevsky — /The Brothers Karamazov/

The Grand Inquisitor chapter is a standalone essay embedded in a novel.
It works because Dostoevsky trusts the reader to hold the argument in
tension with the surrounding narrative.  Ivan presents the argument;
Alyosha's silence is the counterargument.

** Tolstoy — /Anna Karenina/

"All happy families are alike; each unhappy family is unhappy in its
own way."  This is a thesis statement disguised as an aphorism.  The
novel then proves it by showing that Levin's happiness and Anna's
unhappiness are both more complicated than the opening suggests.

Tolstoy's method is accretion.  He doesn't argue; he accumulates
detail until the pattern emerges.  This is the opposite of synthesis
— it is a deliberate refusal to synthesize, trusting the reader to
find the structure.

** George Eliot — /Middlemarch/

The web metaphor that runs through the novel: every character is
connected to every other through a network of obligations, desires,
and misunderstandings.  Eliot makes the network visible by shifting
perspective constantly.

This is the densely-connected knowledge graph as a literary technique.
Each chapter is a node; the connections are thematic, not hyperlinked.

* Cross-cutting themes

- The relationship between theory and experience (Dostoevsky)
- Accumulation vs. synthesis as modes of understanding (Tolstoy vs. Eliot)
- Networks of meaning that emerge from juxtaposition, not explicit linking
