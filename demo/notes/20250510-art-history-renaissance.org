* Overview

The Italian Renaissance (roughly 1400–1600) was a period of
extraordinary artistic production centred on Florence, Rome, and
Venice.  Its defining characteristic was the rediscovery of classical
antiquity combined with new techniques in perspective, anatomy, and
oil painting.

* Key figures

** Brunelleschi (1377–1446)

Architect.  Built the dome of Florence Cathedral without centering
(temporary wooden support), which was thought impossible.  Also
formalized linear perspective — the technique that allows a flat
surface to represent three-dimensional depth.

** Masaccio (1401–1428)

Painter.  First to apply Brunelleschi's perspective systematically.
The /Trinity/ fresco in Santa Maria Novella is a textbook example:
the barrel vault recedes so convincingly that viewers reportedly tried
to step into it.

** Leonardo da Vinci (1452–1519)

Painter, engineer, anatomist, polymath.  The quintessential
Renaissance figure.  His notebooks — thousands of pages of sketches,
observations, and inventions — are arguably the first personal
knowledge management system.  He cross-referenced constantly,
jumping between anatomy, hydraulics, optics, and painting.

** Michelangelo (1475–1564)

Sculptor, painter, architect, poet.  The Sistine Chapel ceiling is
the most ambitious single artwork ever commissioned.  300+ figures
across 500 square metres, painted over four years largely by one
person on scaffolding.

** Raphael (1483–1520)

Painter.  /The School of Athens/ depicts every major Greek philosopher
in a single architectural space.  It is a visual knowledge graph —
each figure is positioned in relation to others based on their
intellectual affinities.

* Techniques

** Linear perspective
Parallel lines converge at a vanishing point on the horizon.  This
mathematical framework transformed painting from symbolic
representation to spatial illusion.

** Chiaroscuro
The use of strong contrasts between light and dark to model
three-dimensional form.  Leonardo's /sfumato/ technique takes this
further, blurring boundaries to create atmospheric depth.

** Contrapposto
A pose where the figure's weight is shifted onto one leg, creating a
natural asymmetry.  Borrowed from classical Greek sculpture.
Michelangelo's /David/ is the definitive example.

* The workshop model

Renaissance art was produced in workshops (botteghe), not by solitary
geniuses.  A master would design the work; apprentices would prepare
materials, paint backgrounds, and execute secondary figures.  The
master painted the faces and hands.

Attribution is therefore always approximate.  "By Raphael" often means
"designed by Raphael, executed by Raphael and his workshop."  This
collaborative model is closer to modern software development than to
the Romantic myth of the solitary artist.
