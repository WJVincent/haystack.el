#+TITLE: Cons Cells and Lists
#+DATE: 2025-02-05

A cons cell is the primitive building block of Lisp data structures: a pair of
two pointers called the car (first element) and cdr (rest), named after the
registers of the IBM 704 computer. A proper list in Lisp is a chain of cons
cells where the cdr of the last cell is nil: ~(1 2 3)~ is really ~(1 . (2 . (3 .
nil)))~ at the memory level. In emacs-lisp and Common Lisp (cl), ~cons~
constructs a new cell, ~car~ accesses the first element, ~cdr~ accesses the
rest, and ~list~ is a convenience function that chains multiple cons cells.
Dotted pairs ~(a . b)~ are cons cells where the cdr is not a list, used directly
in association lists (alists) where each entry maps a key to a value: ~((key1 .
val1) (key2 . val2))~. Association lists (alists) and property lists (plists)
are two common conventions for key-value storage in Lisp, both built from cons
cells and lists rather than hash tables. In elisp, alist operations like
~assoc~, ~assq~, and ~alist-get~ provide lookup, while ~cl-pairlis~ and ~mapcar~
help transform alists programmatically. Circular lists, created by setting a cdr
to point back to an earlier cell, are possible in Lisp but require care to avoid
infinite loops in traversal code. Understanding cons cells clarifies why many
Lisp operations have O(n) complexity: walking a list means following a chain of
pointers with no random access. The scheme dialect formalizes lists with
predicates ~pair?~, ~null?~, ~list?~ and the same car/cdr accessors,
demonstrating how universal the cons cell abstraction is across Lisp variants.
Mastery of cons cells and lists is the first step toward understanding all Lisp
data manipulation, since alists, trees, and association structures all reduce to
cons cell chains.
