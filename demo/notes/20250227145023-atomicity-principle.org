#+TITLE: The Atomicity Principle in PKM
#+DATE: 2025-02-27
# %%% pkm-end-frontmatter %%%
The atomicity principle in personal knowledge management (pkm) holds that each
note should capture one idea — no more, no less — making it a discrete, self-
contained unit of thought. Atomic notes (zettel) are easier to link to other
notes precisely because they have a single, clear identity; a note about three
things can only be linked awkwardly to other notes. The opposite of an atomic
note is a "brain dump" — a sprawling document containing observations,
questions, and conclusions mixed together in temporal order, which resists
retrieval and connection. In a zettelkasten system, atomicity is what enables
the emergence of unexpected connections: when every note represents exactly one
idea, placing two notes side by side reveals the relationship between two ideas.
The challenge of atomicity is deciding where one idea ends and another begins;
this judgment develops with practice and varies by domain — a note that's atomic
in philosophy might be too broad in mathematics. Note atomicity does not mean
notes must be short — an atomic note about a complex concept may run several
paragraphs as long as every sentence serves the single central claim. Haystack's
search model benefits from atomicity: when notes are well-scoped, a full-text
search via ripgrep (rg) for a concept is more likely to return the specific note
you need rather than a tangentially relevant dump. Org-mode notes can violate
atomicity through heading hierarchies — it is tempting to cram multiple
subtopics under one file when the correct zettelkasten move is to split them
into separate notes. The atomicity principle applies beyond individual notes to
entire knowledge-management workflows: atomic capture, atomic review, and atomic
linking keep each step focused and manageable. Over time, a corpus of well-
atomized notes reveals the genuine structure of your knowledge rather than the
accidental structure of how information happened to arrive.
