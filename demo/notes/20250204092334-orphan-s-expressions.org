#+TITLE: S-Expressions
#+DATE: 2025-02-04

S-expressions (symbolic expressions) are the fundamental syntactic unit of Lisp:
a notation where both code and data are represented as nested parenthesized
lists. The term "s-expression" was coined by John McCarthy in his original 1960
Lisp paper, where symbolic expressions were the primary vehicle for describing
both programs and their inputs. An s-expression is either an atom (a symbol,
number, string, or other primitive) or a pair written as ~(A . B)~ where A and B
are themselves s-expressions. Compound s-expressions read naturally as trees:
~(+ (* 2 3) 4)~ is a tree with ~+~ at the root, ~(* 2 3)~ as the left child, and
~4~ as the right child. The power of s-expressions lies in the fact that code
and data share the same representation — a program is a list, and lists are
first-class data that can be constructed and manipulated at runtime. This
property, called homoiconicity, is the foundation of Lisp macros: a macro
receives its arguments as unevaluated s-expressions and returns a new
s-expression that the evaluator then executes. Editing s-expressions with
structural editors like Paredit or Smartparens enforces balanced parentheses and
enables operations like slurping, barfing, and splicing that move code around by
structure rather than by character. Symbolic expressions appear across the
entire Lisp family: Common Lisp (cl), Scheme, Clojure, and Emacs Lisp all share
the same s-expression syntax at their core. In Emacs, s-expressions are
navigable with ~forward-sexp~, ~backward-sexp~, ~up-list~, and ~down-list~,
making structural movement a first-class editing operation. The simplicity of
s-expressions as a notation — just atoms and nested lists — is deceptive; it is
sufficient to express any computation and has proven remarkably durable across
six decades of Lisp dialects.
