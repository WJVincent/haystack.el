#+TITLE: Retrieval Over Tagging
#+DATE: 2025-03-12
# %%% haystack-end-frontmatter %%%
A recurring insight in personal knowledge management (pkm) is that retrieval
quality matters more than organizational structure: a note you can find is
infinitely more valuable than a perfectly organized note you cannot. Tag-based
and folder-based organization require predicting at write-time which categories
a note will be searched under — but retrieval needs change over time in ways
that are impossible to predict. Full-text search with ripgrep (rg) operates on
the note's actual content rather than on metadata assigned at creation; it finds
notes by what they say rather than by how they were filed. The vocabulary-gap
problem is the fundamental obstacle to tag-based retrieval: the term you use
when searching may not be the term used when writing, and tags bridge this gap
poorly. Haystack's expansion groups are a deliberate answer to the vocabulary-
gap: declaring that "search", "rg", "ripgrep", and "full-text search" are
synonyms ensures that searching for any one finds notes using any other.
Zettelkasten practitioners who rely primarily on search often report that their
notes become more useful over time, not less — because the corpus accumulates
vocabulary density that improves rg recall. Backlink-based retrieval (as in org-
roam) is more precise than full-text search for deliberately connected notes but
requires upfront investment in link creation and maintenance. A combined
strategy: use explicit links for "I know these notes are related and want to
preserve that connection," and use full-text rg search for "I want to see what
I've written about this topic." The second-brain literature is gradually
shifting toward retrieval-centric design: tools like Haystack, Logseq's full-
text search, and Obsidian's quick switcher all emphasize finding over filing.
The meta-skill in knowledge-management is not organizing your notes well — it is
writing notes with enough vocabulary richness that retrieval is reliably
effective.
