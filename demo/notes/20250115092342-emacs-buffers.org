#+TITLE: Emacs Buffers
#+DATE: 2025-01-15
# %%% pkm-end-frontmatter %%%
Buffers are the fundamental unit of work in Emacs; nearly everything you
interact with lives inside one. Unlike most editors that tie buffers directly to
files, Emacs buffers can hold file contents, shell output, compilation results,
or purely in-memory text with no backing file at all. The ~*scratch*~ buffer is
perhaps the most iconic example of a non-file buffer, serving as an interactive
elisp playground from the moment Emacs starts. Every buffer has a major mode
that determines its syntax highlighting, keybindings, and available commands —
switching modes does not create a new buffer, it transforms the existing one.
The buffer-list is maintained as an ordered structure, and the concept of a
buffer-hierarchy becomes relevant when you begin thinking about how parent
frames and child windows relate to the buffers they display. You can have dozens
of buffers open simultaneously without displaying them; most exist quietly in
the background until you summon them via ~switch-to-buffer~ or a completion
frontend like Vertico. Buffer-local variables allow each buffer to carry its own
version of any Emacs setting, which is how major modes customize indentation,
fill-column, and other behavior without affecting the global state. Narrowing a
buffer with ~narrow-to-region~ makes only a portion of the buffer visible and
editable, a powerful technique for focusing on a subsection of a large file. The
~ibuffer~ package provides a full-featured buffer manager displayed in its own
buffer, letting you filter, sort, and operate on groups of buffers at once.
Understanding buffers deeply unlocks a more fluid Emacs workflow — rather than
managing files, you manage a living workspace of named text containers.
