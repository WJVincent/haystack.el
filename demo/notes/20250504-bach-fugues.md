## What is a fugue?

A fugue is a composition built from a single theme (the *subject*)
that enters in different voices at staggered intervals. Each voice is
independent but governed by harmonic rules that constrain how they can
combine.

The Well-Tempered Clavier contains 48 fugues — two in every major and
minor key. They range from simple two-voice fugues to dense five-voice
constructions.

## Structure of a typical fugue

1. **Exposition** — the subject enters in each voice, alternating
   between tonic and dominant. This establishes the theme.
2. **Episodes** — modulatory passages that develop fragments of the
   subject. Often sequential (a pattern repeated at different pitch
   levels).
3. **Middle entries** — the subject returns in new keys.
4. **Stretto** — overlapping entries where voices pile up before the
   previous entry finishes. Creates tension and density.
5. **Coda** — final statement, often with a pedal point.

## Why this matters for thinking about notes

The fugue is a model for how a single idea can be developed through
recombination. The subject is atomic — one melodic idea. Development
happens by placing it in different contexts (keys, inversions,
augmentations), not by adding new material.

This is surprisingly close to how progressive filtering works. You
start with one search term (the subject), then each filter places it
in a new context. The same term means something different when
filtered by "philosophy" vs. filtered by "programming."

## Counterpoint rules

- No parallel fifths or octaves (voices must maintain independence)
- Contrary motion preferred over parallel motion
- Dissonance must resolve stepwise
- Each voice should have a singable contour

These constraints don't limit expression — they generate it. Same
principle as plain-text constraints in note-taking.
