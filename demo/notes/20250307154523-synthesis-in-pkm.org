#+TITLE: Synthesis in PKM
#+DATE: 2025-03-07
# %%% pkm-end-frontmatter %%%
Synthesis is the highest-value activity in personal knowledge management (pkm):
the act of combining ideas from multiple notes or sources into a new claim that
did not exist in any single source. In a zettelkasten second-brain system,
synthesis happens naturally when you browse your notes for a topic and discover
that two zettel, taken together, imply something neither states individually.
The difference between a collection and a knowledge base is synthesis: a
collection stores information, while a knowledge base generates new
understanding through connection and transformation. Writing synthesis notes —
notes that explicitly draw on and cite multiple prior notes — is the most
powerful way to make a zettelkasten productive for original thought and writing.
Haystack's Map of Content (MOC) feature is a synthesis tool: an MOC note
collects and organizes related notes on a topic, and the act of building the MOC
often reveals gaps and connections. The search-centric workflow of Haystack
supports synthesis by making it easy to pull up all notes relevant to a topic
with ripgrep (rg): you see the full landscape of what you know before writing.
Synthesis in emacs pkm is often mediated by org mode: you open several notes in
windows simultaneously, use ~org-insert-link~ to cite them in a new note, and
write the synthesis in the new file. The zettelkasten literature emphasizes that
synthesis notes should be written in your own words, reformulating source
material rather than quoting it — this is what builds genuine understanding. A
corpus without synthesis notes is just an archive; the degree to which synthesis
notes exist and link back to their sources is a reasonable proxy for the health
of a second-brain practice. Regularly scheduled synthesis sessions — blocks of
time devoted to reading across related notes and writing connection notes — are
what separate productive zettelkasten practitioners from those who only
accumulate.
