#+TITLE: Cognitive Load in Knowledge Work
#+DATE: 2025-03-10
# %%% haystack-end-frontmatter %%%
Cognitive-load is the total amount of mental effort being used in working memory
at any given moment; managing it is central to designing effective tools for
knowledge work. Working memory is severely limited — humans can hold roughly 4-7
chunks of information simultaneously — and when cognitive-load exceeds this
capacity, performance degrades sharply and errors increase. The mental overhead
of deciding where to file a note, what to name it, or which tags to assign
consumes working memory that could otherwise be spent on the actual thinking you
came to do. PKM systems that minimize cognitive-load at capture time (low-
friction inboxes, daily notes, quick capture templates) outperform elaborate
systems that require many filing decisions per note. Cognitive-load theory
distinguishes intrinsic load (the inherent complexity of the material) from
extraneous load (difficulty caused by poor tool design or unnecessary complexity
in the workflow). A well-designed search-first system like Haystack reduces
extraneous cognitive-load by eliminating the need to pre-organize notes: you
write, you search, you find — the tool handles retrieval. The mental overhead of
maintaining a complex tagging taxonomy or a carefully curated link graph can
itself become a significant cognitive-load burden that detracts from thinking.
Working memory research supports the idea that offloading information to
external systems (notes, zettel) frees cognitive resources for higher-order
reasoning — this is the fundamental rationale for a second-brain. Interface
design choices accumulate: every extra keypress, every modal dialog, every slow
operation contributes to mental overhead and chips away at flow state. The best
pkm tools are invisible — they impose so little cognitive-load that your
attention stays on ideas rather than on the mechanics of managing them.
