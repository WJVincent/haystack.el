## The Death and Life of Great American Cities (1961)

Jane Jacobs' central argument: cities are problems of organized
complexity, not problems of simplicity or disorganized complexity.
You cannot plan them from above; you must understand them from the
street level.

### Four conditions for city diversity

1. **Mixed uses** — a district must serve more than one primary
   function, preferably more than two. This ensures people are present
   at different times for different reasons.
2. **Short blocks** — frequent turns create more navigation options
   and more corner businesses. Long blocks create dead zones.
3. **Mixed ages of buildings** — a district needs old buildings
   because new ideas need cheap space. All-new districts are
   economically monocultural.
4. **Sufficient density** — there must be enough people to support
   the diversity. Density without diversity is a project; diversity
   without density is a suburb.

### The sidewalk ballet

Jacobs' most famous observation: a city sidewalk is an intricate
network of informal public contact. The ballet of the sidewalk — the
shopkeeper sweeping, children playing, strangers nodding — is not
random. It is an emergent order produced by many independent actors
following simple local rules.

This is navigation without a map. People find what they need through
proximity and familiarity, not through search. The city is legible
because it is dense with signals.

## Connection to information architecture

The analogy to knowledge management is compelling:

- **Mixed uses** → notes that span multiple topics are more valuable
  than pure single-topic notes because they create unexpected
  connections
- **Short blocks** → small, atomic notes with many links create more
  navigation paths than long monolithic documents
- **Mixed ages** → old notes mixed with new ones create temporal
  texture; a corpus of only recent notes lacks depth
- **Sufficient density** → a critical mass of notes is needed before
  search becomes more efficient than browsing

Jacobs would probably distrust any system that tries to organize notes
from above (hierarchical folders, rigid taxonomies). She would prefer
a system where order emerges from the notes themselves — which is
exactly what search-first retrieval does.

## The eyes on the street

Safety in Jacobs' framework comes from natural surveillance — people
watching the street as part of their daily routine. The analogy for
notes: the best way to maintain a knowledge base is not periodic
review sessions but frequent incidental contact through daily search.

Every search is a form of surveillance: you see what's there, notice
what's changed, catch what's broken. Frecency encodes this pattern —
the notes you search most often are the ones you maintain best.
