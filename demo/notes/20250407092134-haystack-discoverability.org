#+TITLE: Haystack Discoverability
#+DATE: 2025-04-07
# %%% haystack-end-frontmatter %%%
Discoverability in Haystack is the probability that a note you need will surface
when you search for it, even if you cannot remember exactly what you wrote or
how you titled it. The three mechanisms that drive discoverability are: rg's
complete recall (it finds all matching files), expansion groups' vocabulary
coverage (it handles synonym gaps), and the ~haystack-describe-discoverability~
command (it classifies every term in a note into Isolated, Sparse, Connected,
and Ubiquitous tiers based on corpus-wide reach). A note is only as
discoverable as its vocabulary: notes that use
multiple related terms for their concepts are found by more queries than notes
that use a single precise term. Writing with discoverability in mind means
including near-synonyms, informal variants, and related terms in note bodies —
not stuffing keywords, but naturally mentioning related concepts as you would in
a well-rounded discussion. The vocabulary-gap problem is the primary threat to
discoverability: if you write "retrieval" now and search for "lookup" in six
months, without an expansion group the note is invisible to that search.
Expansion groups are the configured solution; natural vocabulary richness in
note bodies is the complementary informal solution — and together they provide
high discoverability without constant maintenance. Haystack's MOC feature
improves discoverability at a meta-level: an MOC for "pkm" that links to twenty
related notes creates additional text that rg can match, surfacing the MOC
itself as a hub for the topic. Frecency contributes to discoverability at the
search-chain level: it ranks past search paths by usage so frequently-used
searches are easy to replay via ~haystack-frecent~, reducing the friction of
revisiting familiar territory. The tree view aids orientation: it shows the
forest of active search sessions and their filter chains, helping you retrace
your search path and spot branches you haven't explored yet. Maximizing
discoverability is the primary quality metric for a notes corpus: a note that
cannot be found when needed has no value, regardless of how well-written it is.
