* Core ideas

Stoicism is built on a handful of principles that are easy to state
and hard to practice:

1. Focus on what you can control; release what you cannot.
2. Virtue is the sole good.  Externals (wealth, reputation, health)
   are "preferred indifferents."
3. Live according to nature — meaning reason, since reason is the
   distinguishing capacity of humans.

Marcus Aurelius in /Meditations/ returns to the same themes
obsessively.  Book II alone contains three restatements of the
impermanence argument.  This repetition is not redundancy — it is
rehearsal.  The Stoics understood that knowledge must be practiced,
not merely held.

* Atomicity in Stoic thought

There is an interesting parallel with the atomicity principle in note
taking.  The Stoics decomposed complex situations into individual
judgments that could be examined one at a time.  "It is not the thing
that disturbs you, but your judgment about the thing."  This is
decomposition into atomic propositions — each one testable, each one
revisable.

Epictetus was especially rigorous about this.  The /Discourses/ read
like a curriculum: each chapter isolates one idea and works through it
fully before moving on.  This is the ancient version of "one note, one
idea."

* The discipline of assent

The Stoics had three disciplines: desire, action, and assent.  The
discipline of assent is the most relevant here — it means examining
every impression before accepting it.  Don't react to the raw
stimulus; pause, decompose, evaluate.

This is a retrieval practice.  You are searching your principles for
the relevant one, not browsing a list.  The Stoic sage is someone
whose retrieval is fast and accurate.

* Open questions

- How does Stoic determinism interact with the idea of creative synthesis?
  If everything is fated, what is the point of combining ideas into new ones?
- Is the Stoic emphasis on reason compatible with intuitive, associative
  thinking?  Or is it fundamentally opposed?
