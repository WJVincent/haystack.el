#+TITLE: Spaced Repetition
#+DATE: 2025-03-06
# %%% haystack-end-frontmatter %%%
Spaced repetition is a learning technique that schedules review of material at
increasing intervals, exploiting the "spacing effect" to maximize long-term
retention with minimal review time. The algorithm originally formalized by Piotr
Woźniak as SM-2 (and later SM-18 in the SuperMemo software) schedules each item
based on how easily it was recalled at the last review. Anki is the most widely
used spaced repetition software; it implements a variant of the SM-2 algorithm
and supports text, images, audio, and code snippets as card content. In a
personal knowledge management (pkm) context, spaced repetition complements but
does not replace the zettelkasten: a zettelkasten stores and connects ideas,
while spaced repetition moves them into long-term memory. The org-drill and org-
fc packages implement spaced repetition within Emacs org mode, treating org
headings as cards and scheduling them through the agenda system. A common
criticism of spaced repetition in pkm is that it optimizes for recall of
isolated facts rather than for understanding of connected ideas — which is
precisely what a zettelkasten second-brain is designed to build. Synthesis —
connecting ideas, generating new claims, writing permanent zettel — may do more
for deep understanding than rote review, though the two approaches are
complementary. Creating good spaced repetition cards from notes requires the
same atomicity discipline as writing good zettel: one concept per card, phrased
in a way that tests understanding rather than pattern-matching. The Leitner box
system is a physical analog to spaced repetition: cards are organized into
compartments reviewed at different frequencies based on how often they were
recalled correctly. For Emacs users, integrating spaced repetition through org-
drill with the same notes used in Haystack creates a unified workflow where
notes serve double duty as knowledge-management zettel and review material.
