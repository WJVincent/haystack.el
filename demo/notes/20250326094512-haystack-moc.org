#+TITLE: Haystack Map of Content
#+DATE: 2025-03-26
# %%% pkm-end-frontmatter %%%
A Map of Content (MOC) is a note that serves as a curated index or overview for
a topic, collecting links to related notes (zettel) and organizing them into a
meaningful structure. In Haystack, an MOC is created like any other note via
~haystack-new-note~, but its body consists primarily of links to and summaries
of related notes rather than original observations. The ~haystack-moc~ command
accelerates MOC creation by running a search query and collecting all matching
notes into a template that populates an MOC note with links and titles. MOCs sit
one level above atomic notes in the zettelkasten hierarchy without imposing a
folder structure: they are discovery and navigation aids, not containers. A
well-maintained MOC for a topic like "emacs pkm" might link to notes on org-
roam, Haystack, org-mode, Consult, and Vertico, providing a starting point for
anyone (including your future self) new to the topic. The act of building an MOC
is itself a synthesis exercise: you must decide which notes belong, how to group
them, and what the relationships between them are — insights that often surface
while assembling the list. MOCs in Haystack benefit from expansion groups: if
your MOC is titled "PKM" but some constituent notes use "zettelkasten" or
"second-brain," Haystack's expansion ensures searches still find them together.
The tree view (~haystack-tree-view~) can render an MOC's linked notes as a
collapsible outline, combining the benefits of hierarchical browsing with the
flat-file storage model. MOCs should be revisited and updated as new notes
accumulate: a stale MOC is worse than no MOC because it creates a false sense of
completeness. For large corpora, having MOCs for each major topic area is the
difference between a searchable archive and a navigable knowledge system.
