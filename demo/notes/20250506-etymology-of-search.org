#+title: Etymology of Search
#+date: 2025-05-06
%%% haystack-end-frontmatter %%%

* The word "search"

From Old French /cerchier/ (to search, seek), from Latin /circare/
(to go around, traverse), from /circus/ (circle, ring).

So "to search" originally meant "to go around in circles."  There is
an irony here: the most efficient retrieval method in computing is
named after the least efficient physical strategy.

The shift from circular traversal to targeted lookup mirrors the
shift from browsing to searching in knowledge management.  You used
to wander the stacks of a library.  Now you type a query.

* "Haystack"

The idiom "needle in a haystack" dates to at least the 16th century.
The metaphor assumes that finding is hard because the target is small
and the search space is large.

But the metaphor is wrong for text search.  In a haystack of notes,
you are not looking for one needle — you are looking for every piece
of straw that matches a description.  The operation is filtering, not
finding.  Ripgrep does not find a needle; it removes all the non-matching
hay.

* "Retrieval"

From Old French /retrouver/ — to find again.  The prefix /re-/
implies that you once had what you are looking for.  Retrieval is
re-finding, not first-finding.

This maps well to frecency.  You search for things you have searched
for before.  The system remembers your retrieval patterns and
optimizes for re-finding.

* "Filter"

From Medieval Latin /filtrum/ (felt), used as a straining material.
To filter is to pass through a medium that retains some things and
lets others through.

Progressive filtering in Haystack is literal: each filter step is a
felt layer that catches a different kind of noise.  What passes
through all layers is signal.

* "Note"

From Latin /nota/ — a mark, sign, or annotation.  Originally a mark
in the margin, not a standalone document.  The evolution from
marginal annotation to standalone note is the evolution from
context-dependent to context-free knowledge.
